<ProblemBlock>

Most Learning & Development (L&D) teams know bad training when they see it. They watch a learner click through a course, sigh audibly, then walk back to their desk and ask a colleague the same question they were just "trained" on. The team feels the problem, but they can't name it.

Without a shared vocabulary for *why* something doesn't work, design conversations devolve into preference fights. *"I think the slides need more color." "I think there should be a quiz."* Stakeholders pile on requests because the only thing they can articulate is *more*. The result is design-by-committee.

</ProblemBlock>

I built a 60-minute conference session for instructional designers, pitched at the part of the room that's already opinionated but stuck. The session connects cognitive science to learner-centered creativity through five of Richard Mayer's multimedia learning principles, with examples drawn from the kinds of bad training the audience deals with every week.

The session is structured around a single line I wanted the audience to repeat afterward: *every design choice has a cost, and the work is naming which cost is worth paying.*

I designed a take-home handout to extend the session's life beyond the room: a printable checklist version of the framework, organized into Designing for Clarity, Designing for Balance, and Designing for Connection. The point was to give attendees something they could actually use in their next design review on Monday, not just a slide deck PDF.

<LessonsBlock>

A conference session is a different medium than a course. The same framework needs different scaffolding: less depth, more memorable framing, room for the audience to react. The next time I present this, I'd cut one of the five principles and spend the recovered time on a single live design critique with the audience. The principles work, but watching them get applied is what makes them stick.

</LessonsBlock>
