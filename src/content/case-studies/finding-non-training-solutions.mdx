<ProblemBlock>

Central Operations functions more like a corporate office than the warehouses my team usually supports, but they were being on-boarded with the same generic warehouse training as everyone else. Stakeholders said the obvious things: it was too long, and it wasn't relevant to the actual job.

The ask was a shorter, more relevant training. My first question was the same one I always start with: is training the right intervention here? Before I cut a slide or recorded a video, I wanted to see the real gap.

</ProblemBlock>

<ProcessBlock>

I ran a Performance Gap Analysis (PGA). Current behavior versus expected behavior, triangulated from stakeholder input. The goal was to see what was actually driving the underperformance, not what stakeholders *said* was driving it.

Then learner profiles. I interviewed subject-matter experts and Tier 1 associates to understand the day-to-day: what they look up, what they get stuck on, what their managers correct most often. The gap was mostly *environmental*. Missing job aids, unclear expectations, no signals or consequences when steps got skipped. Not a skill or knowledge gap. So most of the fix wasn't going to be training.

I designed the system of non-training supports first (the job aids, the signals, the cadence), and *only after that* trimmed the onboarding course to cover what was genuinely a knowledge gap. I collaborated with Workplace Health and Safety to keep the regulatory content verbatim while cutting everything that could live in a job aid instead.

Total time-in-training per new hire dropped fourteen percent (about twenty-five minutes), even after we *added* new role-specific content. The onboarding course alone shrank from fifty-six minutes to eighteen. The eight work-group intro videos shipped with a mandatory 100% knowledge check, and they double as cross-training when associates move between groups, so there's no separate cross-train event needed.

The non-training supports (job aids, expectation cadence, environmental signals) absorbed the bulk of the original "training gap" without requiring a single new course.

</OutcomeBlock>

<LessonsBlock>

Training was the requested artifact, not the real solution. The only reason this worked is because the performance gap analysis ran *before* anyone built anything. If I'd jumped straight to "make the course shorter and more relevant," I would have shipped a faster version of the wrong intervention.

</LessonsBlock>
