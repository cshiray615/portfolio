<ProblemBlock>

This might feel familiar.

You've inherited a Storyline file system that made no sense before you got there. 200 files, each translated into five different languages, buried in nested folders passed down through generations of designer. It's a mess, and your team doesn't have the time or budget to keep up with it.

Then a stakeholder asks: *"We need to find everywhere we use the word 'client' in our training and change it to 'defendant.'"*

That's when you realize your content isn't just messy. It's *unmanageable*. The audit work is the gating step before any meaningful redesign can start. It eats weeks of designer time, and because it's manual, it tends to be incomplete, inconsistent across reviewers, and immediately stale.

Sure, you could pay hundreds of thousands a year for a Learning Content Management System (LCMS). Most Learning & Development (L&D) teams don't have that kind of budget.

That's where AI comes in. A ChatGPT subscription is a tiny fraction of an LCMS, and most of us already have access. Instead of paying for expensive tooling, we get AI to do the heavy lifting. Less time filing, more time designing.

</ProblemBlock>

The .story format is a zipped archive of XML, audio, and assets. Extracting structured content from it doesn't require Articulate's runtime, just the right parser and some patience.

So I wrote a schema. A small JSON file that maps where the interesting content lives inside a `.story` archive across Storyline versions, with fallbacks for when the content isn't where it usually is. The AI doesn't have to guess.

<CodeSample
  lang="json"
  label="Schema excerpt · slide_title field"
  code={`"slide_title": {
  "priority": [
    {
      "type": "xpath",
      "expr": [
        "//textBox[@name='&Title']//plain/text()",
        "//textBox[@name='&amp;Title']//plain/text()"
      ],
      "notes": "Primary title textbox (v3.x encoding variations)"
    },
    {
      "type": "xpath",
      "expr": ["//textBox[ph/@type='title']//plain/text()"],
      "notes": "Placeholder-typed or legacy naming"
    },
    {
      "type": "heuristic",
      "method": "largest_font_text_run",
      "params": { "top_n": 1, "min_length": 3 },
      "notes": "Font-size based detection when explicit markers missing"
    }
  ],
  "postprocessing": ["strip_whitespace", "merge_adjacent_runs"],
  "implementation": "lxml preferred; ET.find() fallback"
}`}
  caption="Three ways to find a slide title across Storyline versions. Known XPath first, then placeholder-typed XPath, then fall back to 'largest font text run.' The AI tries them in order."
/>

The schema is the easy part. Coding the actual app around it is where it gets interesting. Building this tool meant working with an AI coding agent, and "just vibe with it" doesn't work. You can't accept whatever the model spits out. So I gave the room the framework I'd built for myself: *Vibe Check Coding*.

The team got their pre-design time back, and started using it for the part of the job that actually requires a human (designing).

<LessonsBlock>

The pipeline is the easy part. The harder part is convincing the team that AI-summarized inventory is *trustworthy enough to act on*. Which mostly came down to keeping a designer-in-the-loop review step for any decision that retired or rewrote content. Speed without that step is a liability.

</LessonsBlock>
